Logarithms

Definition

A **logarithm** answers the question: "What **power** must a base number be raised to in order to produce a given number?"

General form: $$\log_b(x) = y \quad \text{means} \quad b^y = x$$ Where $b$ is the base, $x$ is the number (argument), and $y$ is the exponent (logarithm).

The most common bases are **10** ($\log x$), the natural number **$e$** ($\ln x$), and **2** ($\log_2 x$). [Image of Logarithm and Exponential function graph]

Understanding Logarithms

Logarithms are the **inverse function of exponentiation**. They are useful for scaling large numbers and simplifying complex operations:

  • They convert **multiplication into addition**.
  • They convert **division into subtraction**.
  • They convert **powers into multiplication**.

They are widely used in applications involving exponential growth and decay, such as: sound intensity (decibels), earthquake magnitude (Richter scale), and financial calculations.

Key Properties (for base $b > 0, b \ne 1$)

  • **Logarithm of 1:** $$\log_b(1) = 0$$
  • **Logarithm of the Base:** $$\log_b(b) = 1$$
  • **Power Property:** $$\log_b(b^n) = n$$
  • **Domain Restriction:** The logarithm is only defined for $x > 0$. The graph never touches or crosses the y-axis.
  • **Domain and Range:** Domain is $(0, \infty)$, Range is $(-\infty, \infty)$.

Identities

  • **Product Rule:** $$\log_b(a \times c) = \log_b(a) + \log_b(c)$$
  • **Quotient Rule:** $$\log_b(a / c) = \log_b(a) - \log_b(c)$$
  • **Power Rule:** $$\log_b(a^n) = n \cdot \log_b(a)$$
  • **Change of Base Formula:** Allows conversion between bases: $$\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_c(x)}{\log_c(b)}$$ (Most often $c=10$ or $c=e$)

Examples

1. Common Logarithm (Base 10): $$\log_{10}(1000) = 3$$ Because $10^3 = 1000$

2. Binary Logarithm (Base 2): $$\log_2(8) = 3$$ Because $2^3 = 8$

3. Applying the Power Rule: $$\log(100) = \log(10^2) = 2 \cdot \log(10) = 2 \cdot 1 = 2$$
